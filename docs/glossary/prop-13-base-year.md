# Prop 13 base year

> The year your home's assessed value was last reset. Resets only on change of ownership or new construction.

Under Proposition 13 (CA Constitution Article XIIIA), your home's **assessed value** — the number the county multiplies by ~1.16% to compute your tax bill — is set in your **base year** and grows at no more than 2%/year thereafter, **unless** one of two things happens:

1. **Change of ownership.** The home is sold (or transferred outside narrow family-transfer exceptions). The new owner gets a new base year at the purchase price.
2. **New construction.** A teardown-and-rebuild, an ADU, or a major remodel triggers a partial reassessment for the new portion only.

The base-year rule is what makes California so unusual: long-time owners can pay 1/3 to 1/5 the property tax of their next-door neighbor on an identical home, just because they bought first. This is also the reason **moving within California is structurally painful** — even a same-priced lateral move resets your assessed value to today's market price.

### Carrying your base year (Prop 19)

Proposition 19 (2020) modified the rules:

- **Homeowners 55+, severely disabled, or wildfire-displaced** can transfer their *current* assessed value to a new principal residence, anywhere in California, up to three times in their lifetime. (Replaces the older Prop 60/90 rules.)
- **Inherited homes** no longer carry the parent's base year unless the child uses the home as their primary residence (the old "intergenerational transfer" loophole closed).

For first-time home buyers under 55, Prop 19's portability doesn't apply — your base year is the year you buy, full stop.

### Concrete example

A 1995-built Fremont home was bought new for $250K. By 2026 it would have an assessed value of $250K × 1.02³¹ ≈ **$465K**, so the original owner's tax bill is about **$5,400/year**.

You buy that home in 2026 for **$1.5M**. Your new base-year value is $1.5M, your year-1 tax is **$17,400**, and your year-30 tax (if held) is $1.5M × 1.02³⁰ ≈ **$31,500/year**.

**The seller's $5,400/year tax bill will not transfer to you.** Don't trust public-records "current tax" numbers when comparing properties — they tell you the *seller's* tax, not yours.
