# Mello-Roos

> A special tax that funds infrastructure in newer subdivisions. Common in Tri-Valley, rare in older Bay Area neighborhoods.

When a developer builds a new subdivision, someone has to pay for the streets, sewers, schools, and parks. Before 1978's Proposition 13, that came out of property-tax revenue. After Prop 13, that math broke. The Mello-Roos Community Facilities Act of 1982 created a workaround: a **Community Facilities District (CFD)** can levy a special tax on each parcel to fund the infrastructure, in addition to the regular property tax.

If you're house-shopping in **Dublin, Pleasanton, San Ramon, Mountain House, Brentwood, Tracy, parts of Livermore, or any subdivision built after ~1985**, expect to see Mello-Roos on a lot of listings. Older neighborhoods (most of Fremont, Sunnyvale, Berkeley, Oakland) generally don't have it.

A few things to know:

- **The amount is fixed for the life of the bond** — typically 25–40 years from the district's formation. After that it ends. Look up the CFD's expiration on the county's "special taxes" lookup.
- **It's per-parcel**, not per-purchase-price. The same neighbor might pay $4,000/yr while a slightly bigger lot pays $5,000.
- **It can compound 2%/yr** in some districts (look at the formation documents).
- **It's not deductible** as state-and-local-tax in most cases, since it's a special assessment, not an ad-valorem tax.

### Concrete example

A 2018-built home in Dublin's Boulevard subdivision typically carries about **$4,500/year in Mello-Roos** with a 2030 expiration. On a $1.4M home, that's roughly **$375/month** added to your housing cost — and worth modeling explicitly because it disappears in ~5 years.

When we don't have parcel data we use an area-typical estimate. The monthly-cost breakdown surfaces it on its own line so you can see exactly what's modeled.
