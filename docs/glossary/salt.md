# SALT cap ($10K)

> The federal limit on how much state-and-local tax you can deduct on Schedule A. Painful for California homeowners; doesn't change *what* you pay, just what you can write off.

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 capped the **state-and-local-tax (SALT) deduction** at **$10,000 per return** (so still $10K for married-filing-jointly, not 2 × $10K). The cap covers the total of:

- State income tax
- Local property tax
- Sales tax (if you elect that instead of income tax)

Before 2018, a high-income California homeowner could deduct all of their state income tax *plus* their full property tax bill. Post-cap, you typically max out the $10K just from state income tax alone — meaning **your property-tax deduction is effectively zero on the federal return.**

This matters for the True-Cost-of-Ownership math: don't assume "I'll write off my property tax" the way a 2010 retirement-planning article said you could.

The SALT cap is **per year** — you don't aggregate. The mortgage-interest deduction is separate (capped at the first $750K of loan principal for loans originated 2018+) and **is not** capped by the $10K SALT limit.

The cap is currently scheduled to be a permanent part of the tax code as of 2026. Future legislation could change it; we'll update this entry when that happens.

### Concrete example

You earn $400K in California with a $1.5M Fremont home.

- State income tax (CA top marginal): roughly $30,000.
- Property tax (Alameda effective ~1.16%): roughly $17,400.
- **SALT-deductible total: $10,000** (the cap).

You "lose" the federal benefit of $37,400 in actual taxes paid. Your federal tax bill is ~$10K higher than the pre-2018 world would have produced. We surface this honestly in the TCO breakdown — over a 10-year hold the SALT cap costs roughly $100K compared to a counterfactual without it.

Source: [26 USC 164(b)(6)](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/164).
